The State of Cancer

Twenty years of cancer data reveal where progress happened, where it stalled, and who was left behind. The dashboard begins with the broad picture across cancers and sexes in Canada, then narrows to colorectal cancer as a case study in how screening and treatment shaped outcomes worldwide.

01 · Canadian Landscape
Where Progress Stalled

Across major cancer sites in Canada, incidence and mortality followed different paths between 1997 and 2017. In some cases, mortality declined while incidence remained stable or increased, while in others both measures changed in similar directions. Differences between males and females add another layer of variation to these patterns.

The overall pattern is uneven rather than uniformly improving. Declines in cancers such as lung and stomach contrast with limited change or worsening trends in liver and pancreas.
02 · Sex Disparities
Men and Women Responded Differently

Changes in cancer mortality over time differ not only by cancer type but also by sex. Some cancers show similar trajectories for males and females, while others separate more clearly over time.

Improvement is not evenly distributed. In cancers such as lung and liver, the gap between males and females is substantial, while in others it remains relatively small.
Why colorectal cancer? Because it is where the data shows the clearest case study in how screening, treatment, and timing shaped outcomes differently across countries.
03 · Global Comparison
Who Improved and Who Worsened

Colorectal cancer outcomes between 1997 and 2017 vary widely across countries, with both incidence and mortality showing large changes in different directions. The comparison highlights where outcomes improved the most and where they worsened across both measures.

04 · Global Incidence
Diverging Trajectories

When colorectal cancer incidence is measured relative to each country's 1997 baseline, long-term trajectories become easier to compare despite different starting levels. Over time, these trajectories separate into distinct patterns across countries.

Divergence becomes more pronounced after the mid-2000s. Some countries continue to decline, while others increase, widening the gap between outcomes.
05 · Head to Head
The Screening Divide

Canada and Australia began with similar colorectal cancer incidence and mortality levels, but their trajectories separate over time. The vertical markers indicate key milestones, including the introduction of treatments such as FOLFOX and bevacizumab, as well as the rollout of organized screening programs in Australia and Alberta, which provide context for how these trends evolved.

The separation is most visible in mortality. Australia shows a sharper decline, while Canada's reduction is more gradual, with the timing of screening aligning with these differences.
In Summary
Progress Is Real but Uneven, and the Gaps Are as Important as the Gains
At first glance, the data show progress. Colorectal cancer mortality fell in many countries, and several cancers in Canada declined over two decades. But the closer you look, the more the gaps define the story. Australia and Canada started from similar positions, yet screening timing alone opened a widening divide. Within Canada, lung and stomach cancer mortality dropped while liver cancer more than doubled. And even where mortality fell, it did not fall the same way for men and women. What emerges is not a story of failure or success, but of uneven reach, where breakthroughs arrived for some cancers, some countries, and some populations, but not for others.
Key Terms
Incidence — the number of new cancer diagnoses per year in a population.
Mortality — the number of deaths from a specific cancer per year in a population.
ASR (Age-Standardized Rate) — a way of counting cancer cases or deaths that removes the effect of age differences between populations, so you can compare a younger country to an older one on equal footing. Expressed per 100,000 people.
All rates in this dashboard are ASR per 100,000 unless otherwise noted.
